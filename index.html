Vilnius — March Temperature Anomaly (30 years)

Daily mean temperature vs. 30-year baseline, March 1–16 each year. Red = warmer than baseline · Blue = cooler · Outlined bar = current year.

💡 How to read this: Each bar shows how much warmer (🔴 red) or cooler (🔵 blue) March was compared to normal. "Normal" (the baseline) is the average March temperature calculated from the previous 30 years of data. Think of it like your height compared to other kids your age — if you're taller than average, the number is positive; shorter, it's negative. A value of +2 °C means that March was 2 degrees warmer than a typical March.

Regional Monthly Anomalies — Apache Beam

Month-by-month temperature anomaly (°C vs 1991–2025 baseline) for Lithuanian and neighbouring cities. Computed by an Apache Beam pipeline. Select a city to view its year × month heatmap.

💡 How to read this table: Each cell shows how much warmer (red) or cooler (blue) that month was compared to normal. The baseline (normal) is the average temperature for that month, calculated across all years from 1991 to 2025. Imagine measuring your body temperature every day for 30 years and calculating the average — that average is the baseline, and the anomaly tells you how far today's temperature is from it. A cell showing +3.5 means that month was 3.5 °C warmer than the long-term average. Hover over any cell to see the exact value and its z-score (how unusual this is statistically).

Known Project Limitations

This dashboard is a portfolio MLOps demonstration. The following weaknesses should be considered before using results for any operational or publication-quality analysis.

  • Reanalysis data, not station records. All temperature values come from the ERA5 reanalysis grid (0.25° ≈ 28 km resolution). ERA5 interpolates and models the atmosphere from satellite and radiosonde observations — it is not the same as a thermometer on the ground. For authoritative Lithuanian climate statistics, cross-validate against the Lithuanian Hydrometeorological Service (LHMT) station network.
  • Single data source. The entire pipeline depends exclusively on the Open-Meteo ERA5 API. Any API outage, schema change, or rate-limit interruption will stall all Airflow DAGs simultaneously. There is no fallback or mirror source.
  • Baseline period is not fully standardised. The Vilnius March chart uses a rolling 30-year window, while the city YTD comparison and Beam heatmap are pinned to 1991–2025. These different reference periods produce subtly different "normals" and are not directly comparable.
  • Simple ML model with limited features. The PyTorch MLP uses only day-of-year and year as inputs. It cannot capture drivers such as the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO), El Niño/La Niña (ENSO), or soil-moisture feedbacks, which strongly influence Lithuanian temperature variability.
  • No uncertainty quantification. Anomaly values and model predictions are reported as point estimates with no confidence intervals, credible intervals, or ensemble spread. A single number without error bars can be misleading.
  • Small geographic scope. Analysis covers Lithuania and a handful of nearby cities. Results should not be extrapolated to broader regional or global conclusions.
  • ERA5 data latency. ERA5 reanalysis is typically available with a ~5-day delay. "Latest" values on this dashboard may not reflect the most recent days and should not be treated as real-time observations.
